# Carnitas (Mexican Slow-Braised Pulled Pork)

**Cuisine:** Mexican | **Difficulty:** Medium | **Time:** 3 hrs 50 min | **Serves:** 8

Carnitas — meaning "little meats" in Spanish — is one of Mexico's greatest contributions to the world of slow-cooked pork. The meat is braised low and slow in its own rendered fat until it becomes impossibly tender, then crisped at the end to create a contrast of textures: silky, pull-apart interior with golden, caramelized edges.

## Background & Origins

Carnitas originated in the state of Michoacán in central-western Mexico, where it has been a staple of family gatherings, market stalls, and celebrations for centuries. Traditional preparation involves submerging large cuts of pork in rendered lard inside a copper pot called a cazo, cooking it slowly over a wood fire until the meat is fall-apart tender.

The technique bears striking similarities to French confit, where duck legs are slow-cooked in their own fat — both methods use fat as a cooking medium to achieve extraordinary tenderness while developing deep, complex flavors. This connection between Mexican and European braising traditions illustrates how slow-fat-cooking is a universal culinary principle.

In modern Mexican cooking, carnitas are most commonly served in tacos, but they also appear in burritos, tortas (sandwiches), tamales, and alongside rice and beans as a plated meal. Every region and family has their own variation — some add Coca-Cola for sweetness, others use milk for tenderness, and some swear by adding a whole orange to the braising liquid.

## Ingredients

### For the Pork
- 4 lbs boneless pork shoulder (also called pork butt), cut into 3-inch chunks
- 1 tablespoon kosher salt
- 1 teaspoon freshly ground black pepper
- 2 teaspoons ground cumin
- 1 teaspoon dried Mexican oregano
- 2 tablespoons lard or vegetable oil

### For the Braising Liquid
- 1 medium white onion, quartered
- 6 garlic cloves, smashed
- 2 jalapeño peppers, halved and seeded
- 2 bay leaves
- 1 cup fresh orange juice (about 2 oranges)
- 2 tablespoons fresh lime juice
- 1 cup water
- 1 cinnamon stick (optional, for Michoacán-style)

### For Serving
- Warm corn tortillas
- Diced white onion
- Fresh cilantro leaves
- Lime wedges
- Salsa verde or salsa roja

## Instructions

1. **Season the pork.** In a large bowl, toss the pork chunks with salt, pepper, cumin, and oregano. Let sit at room temperature for 15 minutes while you prepare other ingredients.

2. **Sear the pork.** Heat lard or oil in a large Dutch oven or heavy-bottomed pot over medium-high heat. Working in batches to avoid crowding, sear the pork on all sides until deeply browned, about 3-4 minutes per side. Remove and set aside. This step builds the fond (browned bits) that adds tremendous depth of flavor.

3. **Build the braising liquid.** Reduce heat to medium. Add the quartered onion and cook until charred on cut sides, about 3 minutes. Add smashed garlic and jalapeños, stirring for 1 minute. Pour in orange juice, lime juice, and water, scraping up all the browned bits from the bottom of the pot.

4. **Braise the pork.** Return the seared pork to the pot. Add bay leaves and cinnamon stick if using. The liquid should come about halfway up the sides of the meat — add more water if needed. Bring to a simmer, then reduce heat to the lowest setting. Cover and cook for 2.5 to 3 hours, turning the meat once halfway through, until the pork is completely tender and shreds easily with a fork.

5. **Shred and crisp.** Remove pork from the braising liquid and shred with two forks. Discard bay leaves, cinnamon stick, and large onion pieces. You have two options for crisping: (a) Spread shredded pork on a sheet pan, spoon some braising liquid over it, and broil for 4-5 minutes until edges are crispy and caramelized. (b) Heat a cast-iron skillet with a thin layer of lard, add shredded pork in a single layer, and cook without stirring until the bottom is crispy, about 5 minutes, then flip.

6. **Serve.** Pile carnitas onto warm corn tortillas and top with diced onion, cilantro, and a squeeze of lime. Serve with salsa verde alongside.

## Chef's Notes

**The secret is in the crisping.** Many home cooks skip the final crisping step, but it's what transforms good braised pork into transcendent carnitas. The contrast between the caramelized, almost-crunchy edges and the tender interior is what makes this dish special. A cast-iron skillet gives the best results because it maintains consistent high heat.

**Save the braising liquid.** The strained braising liquid is essentially liquid gold — it's deeply flavored pork stock infused with citrus and spices. Use it to moisten the carnitas before serving, as a base for pozole or other soups, or freeze it for future use.

**Substitution notes:** If you can't find Mexican oregano, Mediterranean oregano works but has a slightly different flavor profile — more floral, less earthy. For the citrus, you can substitute tangerine or grapefruit juice for the orange juice to create a slightly different flavor dimension. The jalapeños can be replaced with serrano peppers for more heat.

**Comparing to other slow-pork traditions:** Carnitas shares DNA with American pulled pork (both rely on low-and-slow technique with pork shoulder) and Chinese char siu (both use a combination of sweet and savory flavors with pork). The key difference is the fat-braising technique and the Mexican spice profile of cumin, oregano, and citrus.

## Flavor Profile & Pairings

Carnitas have a rich, porky depth balanced by the brightness of citrus and the warmth of cumin. The caramelized edges add a subtle bitterness that rounds out the dish. This flavor profile pairs beautifully with acidic accompaniments — pickled red onions, salsa verde made with tomatillos, or a simple squeeze of fresh lime. The richness of the pork is also balanced well by the earthiness of refried beans and the neutral base of Mexican rice or warm corn tortillas.

For beverage pairings, carnitas work well with Mexican lager beer, agua fresca (particularly tamarind or hibiscus), or a medium-bodied red wine like Tempranillo.
