# Baked Mac and Cheese

**Cuisine:** American Comfort | **Difficulty:** Easy | **Time:** 50 min | **Serves:** 8

Baked mac and cheese is the undisputed king of American comfort food — tender pasta enveloped in a rich, creamy cheese sauce made from scratch with a golden, crispy breadcrumb crust on top. This version uses a combination of sharp cheddar for tang and Gruyère for nutty depth, bound together with a classic béchamel.

## Background & Origins

Mac and cheese has been an American staple since the late 18th century — Thomas Jefferson is famously credited with bringing the recipe back from his travels in France and Italy, where he encountered pasta baked with cheese. The dish evolved from an aristocratic European preparation into America's quintessential comfort food, crossing class and regional lines to become a universal favorite.

The dish's appeal is fundamentally about the same thing that drives Italian carbonara and risotto milanese: coating a starch (pasta, rice) in a rich, creamy, cheese-based sauce. All three traditions use different techniques to achieve creaminess — mac and cheese uses a flour-based roux (béchamel), carbonara uses egg emulsion, and risotto uses starch release (mantecatura). The goal is identical: a cohesive, creamy sauce that clings to every surface.

## Ingredients

### For the Cheese Sauce
- 1 lb elbow macaroni (or cavatappi, shells, or penne)
- 4 tablespoons unsalted butter
- 1/4 cup all-purpose flour
- 3 cups whole milk, warmed
- 3 cups shredded sharp cheddar cheese (about 12 oz)
- 1 cup shredded Gruyère cheese (about 4 oz)
- 1 teaspoon dry mustard powder
- 1/2 teaspoon onion powder
- 1/4 teaspoon cayenne pepper
- 1/2 teaspoon kosher salt
- 1/4 teaspoon freshly ground black pepper

### For the Breadcrumb Topping
- 1.5 cups panko bread crumbs
- 3 tablespoons melted butter
- 1/3 cup grated Parmesan cheese
- 1/2 teaspoon smoked paprika

## Instructions

1. **Cook the pasta.** Boil macaroni in heavily salted water until 2 minutes short of al dente (it finishes cooking in the oven). Drain and set aside. Do not rinse — the surface starch helps the sauce adhere.

2. **Make the roux.** In the same large pot, melt butter over medium heat. Add flour and whisk constantly for 2 minutes until the mixture is golden and smells nutty — this is the roux, and it's the foundation of the sauce. Cooking the flour removes its raw taste and activates its thickening power.

3. **Build the béchamel.** Gradually pour in warm milk while whisking continuously. Add the milk in three additions, whisking until smooth after each before adding more. Bring to a gentle simmer and cook for 3-4 minutes until the sauce thickens enough to coat the back of a spoon. The sauce should be creamy and pourable, not gluey.

4. **Melt the cheese.** Remove from heat. Stir in cheddar and Gruyère in three additions, stirring until each batch is fully melted before adding the next. Removing from heat prevents the cheese from seizing or becoming grainy. Add mustard powder, onion powder, cayenne, salt, and pepper.

5. **Combine.** Fold the cooked macaroni into the cheese sauce until every piece is coated. Pour into a buttered 9x13 baking dish.

6. **Make the topping.** Toss panko with melted butter, Parmesan, and smoked paprika. Scatter evenly over the mac and cheese.

7. **Bake.** Bake at 375°F for 25-30 minutes until bubbling around the edges. Switch to broil for the last 2-3 minutes to get the breadcrumb topping golden and crispy. Watch carefully — it goes from golden to burnt quickly.

8. **Rest and serve.** Let cool for 10 minutes. The sauce will thicken slightly as it cools, going from pourable to perfectly scoopable.

## Chef's Notes

**Why two cheeses.** Sharp cheddar provides the tangy, classic mac-and-cheese flavor that everyone expects. Gruyère adds nutty complexity, superior melting properties, and prevents the sauce from being one-note. This two-cheese approach is similar to how Italian dishes combine Parmesan (sharp, salty) with mozzarella (creamy, mild) — each cheese contributes something different.

**The roux is essential.** Without the flour-butter roux, there's nothing to stabilize the cheese sauce — it would separate into greasy, clumpy cheese and thin liquid when baked. The roux acts as an emulsifier, binding fat and liquid into a smooth, stable sauce. This is the same principle behind any béchamel-based dish.

**Warm the milk first.** Adding cold milk to a hot roux creates lumps. Warming the milk ensures smooth integration. This is a basic French sauce technique that applies to any roux-based sauce.

**Mustard powder is the secret weapon.** It doesn't make the mac and cheese taste like mustard — it enhances the cheese flavor, adding a subtle sharpness and depth. It's been a standard ingredient in mac and cheese since the earliest American recipes and appears in nearly every professional version.

**Connection to other cheese-and-starch traditions.** Mexican enchiladas bake cheese and starch in sauce. Italian eggplant parmigiana layers cheese with a baked starch. Chinese dan dan noodles coat noodles in a rich, creamy sauce. The universal principle: people love starch coated in rich, creamy, savory sauce, and every cuisine finds its own way to achieve it.

## Flavor Profile & Pairings

Baked mac and cheese is rich, tangy, and deeply savory. The cheddar provides a sharp, assertive cheese flavor, while the Gruyère adds nutty sweetness. The béchamel base is creamy and comforting, and the breadcrumb crust adds a satisfying crunch and buttery contrast to the soft interior.

This is the classic side dish for American BBQ: smoked brisket, pulled pork sandwiches, or buttermilk fried chicken. The richness pairs well with sharp, acidic sides like coleslaw, pickles, or a vinegar-dressed green salad. Collard greens or steamed broccoli add a green element. For beverages, an IPA, sweet tea, or lemonade all complement the richness.
