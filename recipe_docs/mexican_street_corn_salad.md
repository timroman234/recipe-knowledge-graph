# Mexican Street Corn Salad (Esquites)

**Cuisine:** Mexican | **Difficulty:** Easy | **Time:** 25 min | **Serves:** 6

Esquites is the deconstructed, spoonable version of elote — Mexico's famous grilled street corn on the cob. Charred corn kernels are tossed with a creamy, tangy dressing of mayo, crema, and lime, then showered with salty cotija cheese and spicy chile powder. It is simultaneously rich, bright, spicy, and refreshing.

## Background & Origins

Street corn in Mexico comes in two forms: elote (corn on the cob, slathered with toppings and eaten on a stick) and esquites (cut kernels served in a cup with the same toppings). Both are ubiquitous at street carts, market stalls, and festivals across Mexico, but esquites is more practical for serving at home and easier to eat.

The combination of charred corn with creamy dairy and sharp chile is addictive because it hits every flavor note simultaneously: sweet (corn), savory (cheese), sour (lime), spicy (chile powder), rich (mayo and crema), and smoky (char). This same principle — sweet vegetable + creamy dressing + sharp accent — appears in American comfort food (creamed corn, coleslaw) and Italian preparations (corn with Parmesan and basil).

## Ingredients

- 6 ears fresh corn, kernels cut off (about 6 cups), or 6 cups frozen corn, thawed
- 2 tablespoons butter
- 1 jalapeño, seeded and minced
- 2 garlic cloves, minced
- 1/3 cup mayonnaise
- 1/4 cup Mexican crema or sour cream
- 1/2 cup crumbled cotija cheese (plus more for garnish)
- 3 tablespoons fresh lime juice (about 2 limes)
- 1 teaspoon ancho chile powder (or Tajín seasoning)
- 1/2 teaspoon smoked paprika
- 1/4 cup fresh cilantro, chopped
- Kosher salt to taste

## Instructions

1. **Char the corn.** Melt butter in a large cast-iron skillet over high heat. Add corn kernels in a single layer (work in batches if necessary). Let the corn sit undisturbed for 2-3 minutes until it develops dark char marks — resist the urge to stir. Toss and repeat until kernels are charred in spots all around, about 6-8 minutes total. The key is high heat and patience.

2. **Add aromatics.** Reduce heat to medium. Add minced jalapeño and garlic, stir for 1 minute until fragrant. Remove from heat and let cool for 5 minutes — you don't want the heat to break the dressing.

3. **Make the dressing.** In a small bowl, whisk together mayonnaise, Mexican crema, lime juice, chile powder, and smoked paprika.

4. **Combine.** Pour the dressing over the warm corn and toss to coat evenly. Fold in cotija cheese and cilantro. Taste and adjust salt and lime.

5. **Serve.** Transfer to a serving bowl. Top with additional crumbled cotija, a sprinkle of chile powder, and cilantro leaves. Serve warm or at room temperature — both are excellent.

## Chef's Notes

**Cast iron is essential for charring.** The high heat retention of cast iron creates the Maillard reaction on the corn kernels — those dark, sweet-smoky char marks are what elevate this from a corn salad to street corn. A non-stick pan won't get hot enough. If you have access to a grill, charring whole ears first, then cutting the kernels off, gives even better smoky flavor.

**Cotija is the right cheese.** Cotija is a dry, salty, crumbly Mexican cheese that doesn't melt — it functions similarly to Parmesan or feta. If you can't find it, Parmesan is closer in flavor profile than feta (despite feta being closer in texture). This creates an interesting cross-cultural bridge: the Italian use of Parmesan on vegetables follows the same principle as cotija on corn.

**Scaling and make-ahead.** This salad scales linearly and keeps well refrigerated for 2 days, though the corn loses some of its char crunch. It's an ideal side dish for large gatherings, particularly alongside grilled meats, carnitas tacos, or as part of a taco bar spread.

## Flavor Profile & Pairings

Esquites is a balanced explosion: sweet charred corn, creamy and tangy dressing, salty crumbly cheese, and warm chile heat, all brightened by fresh lime. The smokiness from both the char and the smoked paprika ties everything together.

This pairs naturally with any Mexican main course — carnitas, enchiladas, grilled chicken — but also works well alongside American BBQ like smoked brisket or pulled pork. The creamy, tangy profile complements rich, fatty meats particularly well.
