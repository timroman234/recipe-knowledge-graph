# Chicken Enchiladas with Red Chile Sauce

**Cuisine:** Mexican | **Difficulty:** Medium | **Time:** 55 min | **Serves:** 6

Enchiladas are one of Mexico's oldest and most beloved dishes — corn tortillas dipped in chile sauce, filled with a savory stuffing, and baked until bubbling. This version features a from-scratch red chile sauce made with toasted dried guajillo and ancho chiles, creating a sauce with remarkable depth: smoky, fruity, and mildly spicy.

## Background & Origins

The enchilada dates back to Aztec times, when Mayan people would roll tortillas around small fish and eat them with chile sauce. The word "enchilada" literally means "seasoned with chile" — at its core, this is a dish about the sauce, not the filling. Over centuries, regional variations emerged across Mexico: enchiladas suizas (with cream sauce) in Mexico City, enchiladas verdes (with tomatillo sauce) in central Mexico, and enchiladas potosinas (with ancho chile dough) in San Luis Potosí.

The technique of rolling a filled tortilla and baking it in sauce has parallels in other cuisines — Italian cannelloni and manicotti follow a remarkably similar structural concept, substituting pasta tubes for corn tortillas and béchamel or marinara for chile sauce. Both traditions discovered that wrapping a filling in a starch-based wrapper and baking in sauce creates a dish greater than the sum of its parts.

## Ingredients

### For the Red Chile Sauce
- 6 dried guajillo chiles, stemmed and seeded
- 3 dried ancho chiles, stemmed and seeded
- 2 medium tomatoes, halved
- 1 medium white onion, quartered
- 4 garlic cloves, unpeeled
- 2 cups chicken broth
- 1 teaspoon ground cumin
- 1 teaspoon dried Mexican oregano
- 1 tablespoon apple cider vinegar
- 1 tablespoon vegetable oil
- Salt to taste

### For the Filling
- 1.5 lbs boneless, skinless chicken breast
- 2 cups shredded Monterey Jack cheese (divided)
- 1/2 cup Mexican crema or sour cream
- 1/3 cup diced white onion
- Salt and pepper to taste

### For Assembly
- 12 corn tortillas (6-inch)
- Vegetable oil for softening tortillas
- 1/2 cup crumbled queso fresco
- Fresh cilantro for garnish
- Sliced radishes for garnish

## Instructions

1. **Toast the chiles.** Heat a dry skillet or comal over medium heat. Press the dried chiles flat against the hot surface for 10-15 seconds per side until fragrant and slightly puffed — do not let them blacken or they will turn bitter. Transfer to a bowl, cover with hot water, and soak for 20 minutes until softened.

2. **Char the vegetables.** While chiles soak, place tomato halves, onion quarters, and unpeeled garlic on a sheet pan. Broil for 8-10 minutes, turning once, until charred on both sides. Peel the garlic when cool enough to handle.

3. **Make the sauce.** Drain the chiles, reserving 1/2 cup soaking liquid. Transfer chiles to a blender with charred tomatoes, onion, garlic, chicken broth, cumin, oregano, vinegar, and reserved soaking liquid. Blend until very smooth, about 2 minutes. Heat oil in a saucepan over medium-high heat. Strain the sauce through a fine-mesh sieve into the pan (this removes any tough chile skin). Simmer for 10 minutes, stirring occasionally, until slightly thickened. Season with salt.

4. **Poach the chicken.** Place chicken breasts in a pot, cover with water, add a pinch of salt. Bring to a boil, reduce to a gentle simmer, cover, and cook 15-18 minutes until cooked through (internal temp 165°F). Remove, let rest 5 minutes, then shred with two forks. Toss shredded chicken with 1/2 cup of the red chile sauce, 1 cup Monterey Jack, the crema, and diced onion.

5. **Prepare the tortillas.** Heat 1/4 inch of oil in a small skillet over medium heat. Briefly dip each tortilla in the hot oil for 5-10 seconds per side — just enough to make them pliable, not crispy. Drain on paper towels. Alternatively, wrap tortillas in damp paper towels and microwave for 30 seconds.

6. **Assemble.** Preheat oven to 375°F. Spread 1/2 cup sauce on the bottom of a 9x13 baking dish. Dip each softened tortilla in sauce, fill with about 1/4 cup chicken mixture, roll tightly, and place seam-side down in the dish. Pour remaining sauce over the rolled enchiladas. Top with remaining 1 cup Monterey Jack cheese.

7. **Bake.** Cover with foil and bake 15 minutes. Remove foil and bake 10 more minutes until cheese is melted and sauce is bubbling. Let rest 5 minutes before serving.

8. **Garnish and serve.** Top with crumbled queso fresco, fresh cilantro, and sliced radishes. Serve with Mexican rice and refried beans.

## Chef's Notes

**The sauce is everything.** Don't be tempted to use canned enchilada sauce — the difference between a from-scratch dried chile sauce and a store-bought version is night and day. The dried chiles provide a complex, fruity heat that simply cannot be replicated from a can. Guajillo chiles are mild and fruity; ancho chiles add smokiness and a subtle chocolate-like depth. Together they create a balanced, multi-dimensional sauce.

**Tortilla prep matters.** The quick oil-dip step prevents corn tortillas from cracking when you roll them and also helps them absorb the sauce better during baking. If you skip this, your enchiladas will likely split and the texture will be tough and dry.

**Cheese choices.** Monterey Jack melts beautifully and is mild enough not to compete with the chile sauce. For a more authentic Mexican flavor, substitute Oaxaca cheese (quesillo) or Chihuahua cheese, both of which have excellent melting properties. The queso fresco on top adds a salty, crumbly contrast that shouldn't be melted — add it after baking.

**Comparing layered techniques across cuisines.** The enchilada's structure — starch wrapper, protein filling, sauce, cheese, baked — mirrors Italian eggplant parmigiana (breaded eggplant, marinara, mozzarella, baked) and American baked mac and cheese (pasta, cheese sauce, breadcrumb crust, baked). All three traditions discovered that layering ingredients and baking them creates cohesive, comforting dishes where the sauce binds everything together.

## Flavor Profile & Pairings

The dried chile sauce provides a warm, smoky heat that builds gradually — not sharp or aggressive. The chicken filling is creamy from the Mexican crema and rich from the melted cheese, while the corn tortillas add their distinctive earthy sweetness. The overall flavor profile is deeply savory with a fruity chile backbone.

These enchiladas pair well with cooling sides: a crisp shredded lettuce salad with lime vinaigrette, creamy guacamole, or a simple side of refried pinto beans. Mexican rice made with tomato and chicken broth rounds out the plate. For drinks, a cold Mexican lager, horchata, or an off-dry Riesling cuts through the richness nicely.
