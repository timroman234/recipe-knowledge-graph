# Eggplant Parmigiana (Melanzane alla Parmigiana)

**Cuisine:** Italian | **Difficulty:** Medium | **Time:** 1 hr 25 min | **Serves:** 6

Eggplant parmigiana is southern Italy's answer to lasagna — layers of breaded and fried eggplant slices, marinara sauce, melting mozzarella, and Parmesan, baked until bubbling and golden. It transforms a humble vegetable into a rich, satisfying centerpiece.

## Background & Origins

Despite its name suggesting Parma, eggplant parmigiana likely originated in southern Italy — either Naples, Sicily, or Campania. The name may derive from "parmiciana," the Sicilian word for the wooden slats in a shutter, which resemble the layered arrangement of eggplant slices. Others argue the Parmesan cheese in the dish gives it its name.

The layering technique — protein/vegetable, sauce, cheese, repeated — appears across many cuisines. Mexican chicken enchiladas layer tortillas, sauce, and cheese in a baking dish. American baked mac and cheese layers pasta and cheese sauce. Greek moussaka layers eggplant, meat sauce, and béchamel. All of these traditions discovered that layering and baking creates cohesive dishes where individual ingredients meld into something greater.

## Ingredients

### For the Marinara
- 2 cans (28 oz each) whole San Marzano tomatoes, crushed by hand
- 4 tablespoons olive oil
- 6 garlic cloves, thinly sliced
- 1/2 teaspoon red pepper flakes
- 1 teaspoon dried oregano
- 10 fresh basil leaves, torn
- Salt to taste

### For the Eggplant
- 3 medium eggplants (about 3 lbs total), sliced into 1/3-inch rounds
- 2 tablespoons kosher salt (for drawing moisture)
- 1 cup all-purpose flour
- 4 large eggs, beaten
- 2 cups Italian-style bread crumbs
- Vegetable oil for frying

### For Assembly
- 1 lb fresh mozzarella, sliced thin or shredded
- 1 cup freshly grated Parmesan cheese
- Fresh basil leaves

## Instructions

1. **Salt the eggplant.** Lay eggplant rounds on sheet pans and sprinkle both sides with kosher salt. Let sit 30 minutes — salt draws out bitter moisture and improves the texture during frying. Pat dry thoroughly with paper towels. This step is not optional.

2. **Make the marinara.** Heat olive oil in a large saucepan over medium heat. Add sliced garlic and red pepper flakes, stirring for 1-2 minutes until garlic is fragrant but not browned. Add crushed tomatoes, oregano, and a pinch of salt. Simmer uncovered for 20 minutes, stirring occasionally, until slightly thickened. Stir in torn basil leaves.

3. **Bread the eggplant.** Set up a three-station breading line: flour, beaten eggs, bread crumbs. Dredge each eggplant round in flour (shake off excess), dip in egg, then press into bread crumbs on both sides.

4. **Fry.** Heat 1/4 inch vegetable oil in a large skillet over medium-high heat to 350°F. Fry breaded eggplant rounds in batches, 2-3 minutes per side, until golden brown. Drain on a wire rack set over paper towels. Season with a pinch of salt while hot.

5. **Layer and bake.** Preheat oven to 375°F. Spread 1 cup marinara on the bottom of a 9x13 baking dish. Arrange a single layer of fried eggplant rounds (overlapping slightly is fine). Spoon marinara over the eggplant. Distribute mozzarella slices. Sprinkle with Parmesan. Repeat layers 2-3 more times, finishing with sauce, mozzarella, and Parmesan on top.

6. **Bake.** Cover with foil and bake 25 minutes. Remove foil and bake 15-20 more minutes until cheese is golden and sauce is bubbling vigorously around the edges. Let rest 15 minutes before serving — this allows the layers to set and makes cutting cleaner.

## Chef's Notes

**Salting is the most important prep step.** Unsalted eggplant absorbs oil like a sponge during frying, resulting in greasy, heavy slices. The 30-minute salting draws out moisture and collapses the spongy cell structure, so the eggplant fries up crisp and light while absorbing far less oil.

**Breading technique.** The flour-egg-breadcrumb triple coating creates a barrier that further prevents oil absorption and gives each slice a satisfying crunch that survives baking in sauce. Italian bread crumbs (seasoned with herbs and Parmesan) add extra flavor. This same breading technique is used in American buttermilk fried chicken and Japanese tonkatsu.

**The rest period matters.** Like lasagna, eggplant parmigiana needs 15 minutes after coming out of the oven to set up. Cutting into it immediately results in a saucy, sliding mess. The resting allows the cheese to firm slightly and the layers to hold together when served.

**Lighter alternatives.** For a less labor-intensive version, skip the breading and frying: brush eggplant slices with olive oil and roast at 425°F for 20 minutes per side until golden, then layer and bake as directed. The result is lighter and simpler, though you lose the satisfying crunch of the breaded version.

## Flavor Profile & Pairings

Eggplant parmigiana delivers a harmony of textures and flavors: the crispy breaded coating, the creamy melted mozzarella, the sweet-tangy marinara, and the tender eggplant that has absorbed sauce from both sides. Parmesan adds a salty, nutty depth that ties everything together.

Serve with a simple green salad and crusty bread, or pair with spaghetti dressed in extra marinara for an indulgent Italian-American meal. A medium-bodied Italian red like Chianti Classico or Montepulciano d'Abruzzo pairs beautifully.
