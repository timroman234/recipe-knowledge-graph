# Mapo Tofu (麻婆豆腐)

**Cuisine:** Chinese | **Difficulty:** Easy | **Time:** 25 min | **Serves:** 4

Mapo tofu is Sichuan comfort food at its finest — cubes of silky-soft tofu swimming in a fiery, deeply savory sauce of ground pork, doubanjiang (fermented chili bean paste), and Sichuan peppercorns. The name means "pockmarked grandmother's tofu," honoring the woman credited with creating this dish in 19th-century Chengdu.

## Background & Origins

Created around 1862 at the Chen Xingsheng restaurant in Chengdu by proprietress Chen Mapo, this dish has become one of the most widely recognized Sichuan dishes worldwide. The authentic version is intensely flavored — málà (numbing-spicy) from the Sichuan peppercorns and chili bean paste, with a deeply savory, almost meaty quality from the fermented condiments.

The dish is a brilliant example of how fermented ingredients create umami depth: doubanjiang (fermented chili bean paste), fermented black beans, and soy sauce all contribute layers of savory complexity. This reliance on fermentation for flavor depth connects to many traditions — Italian Parmesan cheese, Mexican aged chiles, and the miso in Japanese cooking all use fermentation to create flavors that cannot be achieved any other way.

Mapo tofu also demonstrates the Sichuan principle of málà — the combination of numbing (má from Sichuan peppercorns) and spicy (là from chili). This dual sensation is unique to Sichuan cuisine and creates a flavor experience that is addictive precisely because it stimulates multiple sensory pathways simultaneously. The heat from chiles overlaps with Mexican cuisine's love of chile-based dishes like chiles rellenos — both traditions build dishes around the transformative power of peppers.

## Ingredients

- 1 lb soft or medium-firm tofu, cut into 3/4-inch cubes
- 6 oz ground pork
- 2 tablespoons doubanjiang (Pixian chili bean paste)
- 1 tablespoon fermented black beans, rinsed and roughly chopped
- 2 tablespoons vegetable oil
- 3 garlic cloves, minced
- 1 tablespoon fresh ginger, minced
- 3/4 cup chicken broth
- 1 tablespoon soy sauce
- 1 teaspoon sugar
- 1 tablespoon Sichuan peppercorn powder (or whole peppercorns, ground)
- 1 tablespoon cornstarch mixed with 2 tablespoons water
- 2 scallions, thinly sliced
- 1 teaspoon sesame oil
- Chili oil to taste (optional)

## Instructions

1. **Prepare the tofu.** Gently slide tofu cubes into a pot of lightly salted simmering water. Let them warm through for 2-3 minutes. This firms the surface slightly so they hold their shape during cooking, and removes the raw bean flavor. Drain carefully.

2. **Cook the pork.** Heat vegetable oil in a wok or large skillet over high heat. Add ground pork and cook, breaking it into small crumbles, for 3-4 minutes until browned and the fat has rendered.

3. **Build the flavor base.** Push the pork to the side. Add doubanjiang and fermented black beans to the oil. Stir-fry for 1-2 minutes until the oil turns red and becomes deeply fragrant — this step is crucial for developing the sauce's color and flavor. Add garlic and ginger, stir for 30 seconds.

4. **Simmer.** Pour in chicken broth and soy sauce. Add sugar. Stir to combine everything with the pork. Bring to a simmer.

5. **Add tofu.** Gently slide the blanched tofu cubes into the sauce. Do not stir aggressively — use a gentle rocking motion of the wok to coat the tofu without breaking it. Simmer for 5 minutes to allow the tofu to absorb the sauce.

6. **Thicken.** Give the cornstarch slurry a stir and drizzle it into the wok while gently rocking. The sauce should thicken to a glossy, clingy consistency within 30 seconds. Add a second round of slurry if needed.

7. **Finish.** Sprinkle Sichuan peppercorn powder over the top. Drizzle with sesame oil and chili oil if desired. Garnish with sliced scallions. Serve immediately over steamed white rice.

## Chef's Notes

**Doubanjiang is the soul of this dish.** Pixian doubanjiang — from Pixian county in Sichuan — is a paste of fermented broad beans and chili peppers, aged for one to eight years. It provides the deep, complex heat and savory backbone. There is no substitute that produces the same result. Look for it at Asian grocery stores or online. It keeps indefinitely in the refrigerator.

**Tofu texture choice.** Soft tofu gives the most traditional silky texture — each cube practically melts in your mouth. Medium-firm holds its shape better for beginners. Never use extra-firm for mapo tofu; it lacks the custard-like quality that makes the dish special.

**Vegetarian adaptation.** Simply omit the ground pork and use vegetable broth. The doubanjiang and fermented black beans provide so much umami that the dish remains deeply satisfying without meat. Some versions add rehydrated dried shiitake mushrooms for additional meaty texture.

**Connection to other spicy-comforting dishes.** The comfort factor of mapo tofu — warm, saucy, served over rice — parallels American mac and cheese (warm, saucy, starchy comfort) and Mexican enchiladas (sauced, warm, deeply flavored comfort). All three are weeknight staples in their home cuisines, dishes that are simple to prepare but deliver maximum satisfaction.

## Flavor Profile & Pairings

Mapo tofu is an assault on the senses in the best way: the doubanjiang provides deep chile heat that builds gradually, the Sichuan peppercorns create their signature numbing tingle on the lips, and the fermented black beans and soy sauce deliver waves of umami. The soft tofu provides a cooling, creamy counterpoint, and the steamed rice absorbs the intensely flavored sauce.

Serve with a cold cucumber salad (smashed cucumbers with garlic, vinegar, and sesame oil) for contrast, or pair with stir-fried greens. Cold beer or warm tea both work well as beverages.
