# Osso Buco alla Milanese (Braised Veal Shanks)

**Cuisine:** Italian | **Difficulty:** Medium | **Time:** 3 hrs | **Serves:** 4

Osso buco — "bone with a hole" — is Milan's gift to the braising world. Cross-cut veal shanks are seared, then slowly braised in wine and broth with aromatic vegetables until the meat is spoon-tender and the marrow inside the bone becomes a buttery delicacy. Finished with gremolata — a bright garnish of lemon zest, garlic, and parsley — the dish balances deep richness with clean freshness.

## Background & Origins

Osso buco is a cornerstone of Milanese cuisine, originating in the Lombardy region of northern Italy. The dish gained international fame through the trattorias of Milan, where it is traditionally paired with risotto alla milanese (saffron risotto), creating one of Italy's most iconic meat-and-starch combinations.

The braising technique used in osso buco — searing meat, building a soffritto (aromatic vegetable base), deglazing with wine, and slow-cooking in liquid — is the same fundamental approach used in Mexican carnitas (braised pork), French coq au vin (braised chicken), and American pot roast. What distinguishes the Italian version is the specific aromatics (soffritto of onion, carrot, celery), the use of wine as a deglazing and braising agent, and the finishing touch of gremolata that lifts the entire dish.

The marrow inside the bone is considered the prize. In Italian dining tradition, a small marrow spoon or fork is provided specifically to extract this rich, gelatinous treasure.

## Ingredients

### For the Osso Buco
- 4 veal shanks, cross-cut 1.5 inches thick (about 12-14 oz each)
- 1/4 cup all-purpose flour (for dredging)
- 3 tablespoons butter
- 2 tablespoons olive oil
- Kosher salt and freshly ground black pepper

### For the Braising Base (Soffritto)
- 1 large onion, finely diced
- 2 medium carrots, finely diced
- 2 celery stalks, finely diced
- 4 garlic cloves, minced
- 1 cup dry white wine
- 1 can (14 oz) crushed tomatoes
- 2 cups chicken or veal broth
- 2 bay leaves
- 3 sprigs fresh thyme

### For the Gremolata
- 3 tablespoons fresh flat-leaf parsley, finely chopped
- Zest of 2 lemons
- 2 garlic cloves, minced

## Instructions

1. **Prepare the shanks.** Pat veal shanks completely dry with paper towels. Season generously on all sides with salt and pepper. Dredge lightly in flour, shaking off excess. Tying kitchen twine around each shank helps them hold their shape during braising but is optional.

2. **Sear the shanks.** Heat butter and olive oil in a large Dutch oven over medium-high heat until the butter foams. Sear the shanks without moving them for 4-5 minutes per side until deeply golden brown. Work in batches if necessary — crowding steams the meat instead of browning it. Remove and set aside.

3. **Build the soffritto.** Reduce heat to medium. Add onion, carrot, and celery to the pot with the residual fat. Cook, stirring occasionally, for 8-10 minutes until vegetables are softened and golden. Add garlic and cook 1 minute more.

4. **Deglaze.** Pour in white wine and scrape up all the fond (browned bits) from the bottom of the pot. Let the wine simmer for 3-4 minutes until reduced by half — this concentrates the flavor and cooks off the raw alcohol.

5. **Braise.** Add crushed tomatoes and broth. Stir to combine. Nestle the seared shanks back into the pot — the liquid should come about two-thirds up the sides of the meat. Add bay leaves and thyme sprigs. Bring to a gentle simmer, then cover with a tight-fitting lid.

6. **Cook low and slow.** Transfer to a 325°F oven (or maintain the gentlest possible simmer on the stovetop). Braise for 2 to 2.5 hours, turning the shanks once halfway through. The meat is done when it is completely tender and pulling away from the bone, and a fork slides in with no resistance.

7. **Make the gremolata.** While the meat finishes, combine parsley, lemon zest, and minced garlic. The raw garlic mellows when sprinkled over the hot dish.

8. **Serve.** Carefully transfer shanks to warm plates or a serving platter. If the braising liquid seems thin, simmer it on the stovetop for 10-15 minutes to reduce and concentrate. Spoon the sauce and vegetables over the shanks. Top generously with gremolata. Serve immediately with risotto milanese or creamy polenta.

## Chef's Notes

**The gremolata is essential, not optional.** It may seem like a simple garnish, but the bright lemon zest and fresh parsley cut through the richness of the braised meat and provide an aromatic freshness that transforms the dish. Without it, osso buco can feel one-note and heavy. This principle — adding a bright, acidic finishing element to a rich braise — appears across cultures: Mexican carnitas get lime and cilantro, American smoked brisket gets pickles and vinegar sauce.

**Soffritto is the Italian mirepoix.** The combination of onion, carrot, and celery (soffritto) is the foundation of Italian cooking, just as mirepoix (the same three vegetables, often with different ratios) anchors French cuisine. The Chinese equivalent is a trinity of ginger, garlic, and scallion. All serve the same purpose: building a complex aromatic base for a braised or stewed dish.

**Veal vs. beef shanks.** Traditional osso buco uses veal, which is more tender and delicate. Beef shanks work well as a substitute — they require 30-60 minutes longer braising time but develop a deeper, beefier flavor. The collagen content in both cuts produces the same silky, body-rich sauce.

**Why slow cooking works.** The shank is a hard-working muscle full of connective tissue (collagen). Long, slow, moist cooking breaks collagen down into gelatin, which gives the braising liquid its luxurious, silky body and makes the meat impossibly tender. This is the same biochemistry at work in Mexican carnitas, American smoked brisket, and Chinese char siu — any cut with lots of connective tissue benefits from extended cooking.

## Flavor Profile & Pairings

Osso buco is deeply savory with layers of flavor: the caramelized seared meat, the wine-enriched tomato sauce, the sweet vegetables, and the bright burst of gremolata. The marrow inside the bone adds a rich, buttery dimension. The dish is hearty without being heavy, thanks to the acidity of the tomatoes and wine.

The classic pairing with risotto milanese (saffron risotto) is one of the great marriages in Italian cuisine — the creamy, golden rice absorbs the braising sauce beautifully. Creamy polenta is an equally excellent choice. A full-bodied Italian red wine like Barolo or Nebbiolo stands up to the richness.
