# Pozole Rojo (Red Pork and Hominy Stew)

**Cuisine:** Mexican | **Difficulty:** Medium | **Time:** 3 hrs 30 min | **Serves:** 8

Pozole is one of Mexico's most iconic soups — a deeply flavored, chile-red broth loaded with tender pork and plump hominy corn, served with a generous spread of fresh toppings. It is ritual food, celebration food, the kind of dish that brings entire families together around a single enormous pot.

## Background & Origins

Pozole has pre-Columbian roots stretching back to the Aztec empire, where it held deep ceremonial significance. The dish centers on hominy — dried corn kernels treated with an alkaline solution (a process called nixtamalization) that causes them to swell, soften, and develop a distinctive chewy texture and nutty flavor. This same nixtamalization process is what transforms raw corn into the masa used for tortillas and tamales.

There are three main varieties of pozole in Mexico: rojo (red, made with dried red chiles), verde (green, made with tomatillos, jalapeños, and pepitas), and blanco (white, with no chile sauce — just the clear pork broth). Pozole rojo is the most popular nationally, particularly associated with the states of Jalisco and Guerrero.

The social tradition around pozole is as important as the recipe itself. It is the customary dish for Mexican Independence Day celebrations on September 15th, and it is common party food — hosts prepare a massive pot and set out bowls of toppings so guests can customize their own bowls. This communal, build-your-own-bowl format shares similarities with Vietnamese pho service and Chinese hot pot traditions.

## Ingredients

### For the Pork Broth
- 3 lbs bone-in pork shoulder, cut into 2-inch chunks
- 1 large white onion, halved
- 1 head garlic, halved crosswise
- 2 bay leaves
- 1 tablespoon whole black peppercorns
- 10 cups water
- 1 tablespoon kosher salt

### For the Chile Sauce
- 8 dried guajillo chiles, stemmed and seeded
- 4 dried ancho chiles, stemmed and seeded
- 4 garlic cloves
- 1 teaspoon dried Mexican oregano
- 1 teaspoon ground cumin
- 1/2 teaspoon ground black pepper

### For the Stew
- 2 cans (29 oz each) white hominy, drained and rinsed (or 1 lb dried hominy, soaked overnight)
- Salt to taste

### Toppings (Serve All — This Is Essential)
- 3 cups finely shredded green cabbage
- 1 bunch radishes, thinly sliced
- 2 avocados, diced
- 1 cup diced white onion
- Fresh cilantro leaves
- Dried Mexican oregano (crushed between fingers)
- Crushed red chile flakes (chile de árbol)
- Lime wedges
- Tostadas or corn tortilla chips

## Instructions

1. **Build the pork broth.** Place pork chunks in a large stockpot. Add halved onion, garlic head, bay leaves, peppercorns, salt, and water. Bring to a boil over high heat. Skim off any foam that rises to the surface in the first 10 minutes — this clarifies the broth. Reduce heat to a gentle simmer, partially cover, and cook for 2 to 2.5 hours until pork is completely tender and falls apart easily.

2. **Toast and rehydrate the chiles.** While the pork simmers, heat a dry skillet over medium heat. Toast the guajillo and ancho chiles for 15-20 seconds per side until fragrant and slightly darkened. Transfer to a bowl, cover with boiling water, and soak for 20 minutes.

3. **Make the chile sauce.** Drain the soaked chiles. Transfer to a blender with garlic cloves, oregano, cumin, black pepper, and 1 cup of the pork broth from the pot. Blend on high for 2 minutes until completely smooth. Strain through a fine-mesh sieve to remove any remaining chile skin — press firmly with a spoon to extract maximum flavor.

4. **Prepare the pork.** Remove the cooked pork from the broth. Discard the onion, garlic head, bay leaves, and peppercorns (strain the broth if needed). Shred the pork into bite-sized pieces, discarding any bones and excess fat. Return shredded pork to the strained broth.

5. **Combine.** Add the strained chile sauce and drained hominy to the pot with the pork and broth. Stir well and bring to a simmer. Cook uncovered for 30 minutes to allow flavors to meld and the hominy to absorb the chile-infused broth. Taste and adjust salt.

6. **Serve.** Ladle pozole into large, deep bowls. Set out all the toppings in separate small bowls so each person can customize their bowl. The traditional approach: add a generous handful of shredded cabbage, several radish slices, a squeeze of lime, a sprinkle of dried oregano crushed between your fingers, diced onion, and a few pieces of tostada broken into the broth for texture.

## Chef's Notes

**The toppings are not optional.** Pozole without its accompaniments is like pho without herbs and bean sprouts — technically the same dish but missing half the experience. The fresh, crunchy toppings provide essential textural and flavor contrast to the rich, warm broth. The shredded cabbage adds crunch, the radishes add peppery bite, the lime adds brightness, and the dried oregano adds an aromatic herbal note when crushed fresh over the hot broth.

**Dried hominy vs. canned.** Dried hominy (available at Mexican grocery stores) has superior texture — chewier, more corn flavor — but requires overnight soaking and 2-3 hours of cooking. Canned hominy is a perfectly acceptable shortcut that saves significant time. If using dried, cook it in a separate pot of salted water until tender before adding to the stew.

**Connection to other stew traditions.** Pozole's structure — long-simmered meat broth with a starch component and fresh toppings — appears across many cultures. Chinese hot and sour soup uses a similar approach of building a complex flavored broth. American clam chowder follows the same logic of a rich, slow-built base with mix-ins. The universality of this "hearty broth + starch + toppings" formula speaks to something fundamental about comfort food across cultures.

**Make-ahead note.** Pozole improves dramatically the next day as the flavors continue to meld. It refrigerates well for up to 5 days and freezes for up to 3 months. The hominy will absorb more broth as it sits, so add extra broth when reheating.

## Flavor Profile & Pairings

Pozole rojo has a warm, earthy chile flavor that is rich without being heavy. The guajillo chiles contribute a fruity, slightly tangy sweetness, while the ancho chiles add smoky depth. The hominy provides a satisfying chewiness and mild corn sweetness that absorbs the chile broth beautifully. The fresh toppings — especially the lime and radish — add bright, sharp counterpoints that keep each bite interesting.

This is traditionally a standalone dish, served in large bowls as a complete meal. Tostadas or tortilla chips on the side are the only essential accompaniment beyond the toppings. For beverages, pozole pairs well with Mexican beer (especially a dark lager like Negra Modelo), mezcal, or agua de jamaica (hibiscus water).
