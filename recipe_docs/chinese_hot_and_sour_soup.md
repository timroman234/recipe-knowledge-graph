# Hot and Sour Soup (酸辣汤)

**Cuisine:** Chinese | **Difficulty:** Easy | **Time:** 35 min | **Serves:** 4

Hot and sour soup is one of China's most beloved comfort soups — a silky, thickened broth loaded with mushrooms, tofu, and pork, made electric with the tang of rice vinegar and the heat of white pepper. It warms you from the inside and clears the sinuses.

## Background & Origins

This soup hails from Sichuan and Hunan provinces, where the balance of sour (suān) and spicy (là) flavors is a foundational cooking principle. Unlike its American-Chinese takeout version (which is often gloopy and overly sweet), authentic hot and sour soup is lighter, more nuanced, and relies on white pepper for heat rather than chili paste.

The concept of a hearty, spiced broth with textural mix-ins appears across many cultures. Mexican pozole rojo follows the same template: a richly flavored broth with meat and starchy elements, served steaming hot. American clam chowder uses a different flavor profile but pursues the same goal of a warming, substantial soup. All three traditions understand that a great soup needs both flavor complexity and textural variety.

The "egg drop" technique — drizzling beaten egg into hot broth to create wispy ribbons — is a Chinese culinary technique that appears in both this soup and in egg drop soup (egg flower soup). The key is to pour the egg in a thin, steady stream while stirring the broth gently in one direction.

## Ingredients

- 6 dried shiitake mushrooms
- 1/4 cup dried wood ear mushrooms (black fungus)
- 6 cups chicken broth
- 4 oz pork loin, cut into matchstick strips
- 1 tablespoon soy sauce (for marinating pork)
- 1 teaspoon cornstarch (for marinating pork)
- 8 oz firm tofu, cut into thin strips
- 1/2 cup bamboo shoots, cut into matchsticks
- 3 tablespoons rice vinegar (or Chinkiang black vinegar)
- 2 tablespoons soy sauce
- 1 teaspoon ground white pepper
- 1 teaspoon sugar
- 3 tablespoons cornstarch mixed with 1/4 cup cold water
- 2 eggs, lightly beaten
- 2 teaspoons sesame oil
- 2 scallions, thinly sliced
- Chili oil to taste

## Instructions

1. **Rehydrate mushrooms.** Soak dried shiitake and wood ear mushrooms in hot water for 20 minutes until soft. Drain (save the soaking liquid for extra flavor), trim stems, and slice thinly. Meanwhile, toss pork strips with 1 tablespoon soy sauce and 1 teaspoon cornstarch.

2. **Build the broth.** Bring chicken broth to a boil in a large pot. Add 1/2 cup of the mushroom soaking liquid. Add sliced shiitake mushrooms, wood ear mushrooms, and bamboo shoots. Simmer for 5 minutes.

3. **Add protein.** Add marinated pork strips, stirring gently to separate them. Add tofu strips. Simmer for 3 minutes until pork is cooked through.

4. **Season.** Add rice vinegar, soy sauce, white pepper, and sugar. Stir and taste — the soup should be assertively sour and peppery. Adjust vinegar and pepper to your preference. The sourness should make you pucker slightly and the pepper should warm your throat.

5. **Thicken.** Give the cornstarch slurry a stir and drizzle it into the simmering soup while stirring. The broth should thicken to a velvety, slightly viscous consistency — not gloopy, but silky enough to coat a spoon lightly. Add more slurry if needed.

6. **Egg ribbons.** While stirring the soup gently in one direction with a chopstick or fork, drizzle the beaten eggs in a thin, steady stream. The egg should form delicate, wispy ribbons throughout the soup. Stop stirring immediately after adding the egg to let the ribbons set.

7. **Finish.** Remove from heat. Drizzle with sesame oil and chili oil to taste. Ladle into bowls and garnish with sliced scallions. Serve immediately while steaming hot.

## Chef's Notes

**White pepper, not black pepper.** White pepper is hotter, more pungent, and more aromatic than black pepper. It provides the distinctive "hot" in hot and sour soup — a sharp, warming heat that's different from chili heat. Don't substitute black pepper; the flavor profile will be completely wrong.

**Vinegar freshness matters.** Add the vinegar near the end of cooking — prolonged simmering dulls its sharp tang. If the soup tastes flat, add another splash of vinegar just before serving. The sourness should be bright and assertive.

**Wood ear mushrooms are for texture.** They have very little flavor but contribute a wonderful crunch-snap texture that contrasts with the silky tofu and broth. They're sold dried at Asian grocery stores and keep indefinitely in the pantry. If unavailable, extra shiitake mushrooms work, though you lose the textural contrast.

**Connection to soup traditions worldwide.** The hot and sour principle — a brothy soup made interesting through the interplay of acid and heat — appears globally. Mexican tortilla soup uses tomato acidity and chile heat. Italian minestrone uses tomato acidity and black pepper. The universal truth: a soup needs contrast and punch to be memorable.

## Flavor Profile & Pairings

Hot and sour soup is a dynamic, constantly shifting experience. The initial impression is of rich, savory broth, followed by the sharp tang of vinegar, then the warm peppery heat that builds. The mushrooms add earthy depth, the tofu provides creamy softness, the pork adds savoriness, and the egg ribbons contribute delicate richness. It's complex without being heavy.

This soup works as a starter before kung pao chicken or dan dan noodles, or as a light meal on its own with steamed rice. Scallion pancakes make an excellent dipping accompaniment. The soup is especially satisfying on cold days or when you're feeling under the weather — the pepper and vinegar are both warming and sinus-clearing.
