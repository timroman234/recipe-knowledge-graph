# Margherita Pizza (Neapolitan-Style)

**Cuisine:** Italian | **Difficulty:** Medium | **Time:** 45 min (plus 24-hr dough rest) | **Serves:** 4

The Margherita is pizza in its purest form: a thin, blistered crust, bright tomato sauce, creamy fresh mozzarella, fragrant basil, and a drizzle of olive oil. Named after Queen Margherita of Italy in 1889, it represents the colors of the Italian flag — red, white, and green — and remains the benchmark by which all pizza is measured.

## Background & Origins

Pizza as we know it originated in Naples in the 18th and 19th centuries as street food for the working poor — flatbread topped with whatever was available: tomatoes, garlic, anchovies, cheese. The Margherita legend holds that pizzaiolo Raffaele Esposito created this specific combination for Queen Margherita Teresa Giovanni during a royal visit to Naples in 1889.

What makes Neapolitan pizza distinctive is its simplicity and the quality demands it places on each ingredient. The dough is just flour, water, salt, and yeast — no sugar, no oil, no enrichments. The sauce is uncooked crushed tomatoes. The cheese is fresh mozzarella, ideally mozzarella di bufala. The baking happens in a brutally hot oven (800-900°F in a wood-fired pizzeria) for just 60-90 seconds.

The principle of "bread base + sauce + toppings" spans cultures: Mexican tlayudas (large crispy tortillas with beans and toppings), Chinese scallion pancakes with dipping sauce, and American comfort food flatbreads all follow the same structural template. But the Neapolitan approach of minimalism and ingredient quality is uniquely Italian.

## Ingredients

### For the Dough (makes 4 pizzas — start day before)
- 500g bread flour (ideally Tipo 00 if available)
- 325ml warm water (about 65% hydration)
- 10g fine sea salt
- 3g active dry yeast (about 1 teaspoon)
- 1 teaspoon olive oil (for bowl)

### For the Sauce (no cooking required)
- 1 can (14 oz) whole San Marzano tomatoes
- 1 teaspoon fine sea salt
- 1 small garlic clove, finely grated (optional)

### For Topping (per pizza)
- 4 oz fresh mozzarella (fior di latte or bufala), torn into pieces
- 5-6 fresh basil leaves
- 1 tablespoon extra virgin olive oil
- Flaky sea salt

## Instructions

1. **Make the dough (day before).** Dissolve yeast in warm water, let stand 5 minutes. In a large bowl, combine flour and salt. Add the yeast water and stir with a wooden spoon until a shaggy dough forms. Turn out onto a clean surface and knead for 10 minutes until smooth, elastic, and slightly tacky. Place in a lightly oiled bowl, cover tightly, and refrigerate for 18-24 hours. This cold fermentation develops complex flavor and better texture.

2. **Divide and proof.** Remove dough from fridge 2 hours before baking. Divide into 4 equal balls (about 210g each). Shape each into a tight ball by tucking the edges underneath. Place on a floured surface, cover with a damp towel, and let rise at room temperature for 2 hours until puffy and doubled.

3. **Make the sauce.** Crush San Marzano tomatoes by hand (or pulse briefly in a blender — you want slightly chunky, not smooth). Stir in salt and grated garlic if using. That's it. Do not cook the sauce.

4. **Preheat.** Place a pizza stone or inverted baking sheet on the top rack of your oven. Preheat to the highest temperature your oven reaches (usually 500-550°F) for at least 45 minutes. The stone must be scorching hot.

5. **Shape the dough.** Working with one ball at a time on a floured surface, press the center with your fingertips, leaving a 1-inch border untouched (this becomes the puffy cornicione/rim). Gently stretch the dough by draping it over your knuckles and rotating, letting gravity do the work. Stretch to about 10-12 inches in diameter. Transfer to a floured pizza peel or parchment paper.

6. **Top and bake.** Spread 3-4 tablespoons of sauce over the dough (less is more — don't overload). Distribute torn mozzarella pieces evenly. Drizzle with olive oil. Slide onto the preheated stone and bake for 8-12 minutes until the crust is blistered and golden, the cheese is melted and spotty, and the bottom is charred in spots.

7. **Finish.** Remove from oven. Immediately top with fresh basil leaves and a final drizzle of olive oil. Sprinkle with flaky salt. Slice and serve within minutes — Margherita pizza does not improve with waiting.

## Chef's Notes

**Cold fermentation is the secret.** The 24-hour cold rise in the refrigerator develops flavor compounds (organic acids and alcohols) that same-day dough simply cannot match. The dough will taste yeasty and one-dimensional without this step. Plan ahead — it's zero effort but takes time.

**San Marzano tomatoes matter.** True San Marzano tomatoes (look for the DOP certification) are sweeter, less acidic, and more meaty than standard canned tomatoes. They are grown in the volcanic soil near Mount Vesuvius, which gives them a distinctive mineral quality. For sauce this simple (literally tomatoes + salt), ingredient quality is everything.

**Mozzarella moisture management.** Fresh mozzarella contains a lot of liquid. Tear it into pieces 15-20 minutes before topping and let it drain on paper towels. Excess moisture on the pizza creates a soggy center — the enemy of good pizza.

**The connection to tomato-based cuisines.** The raw tomato sauce used on Margherita pizza connects to Mexican salsas (also built on raw or lightly cooked tomatoes) and to the importance of tomatoes throughout Italian cooking (marinara sauce for eggplant parmigiana, the base of osso buco braising liquid). Tomatoes arrived in Italy from the Americas in the 16th century and transformed the cuisine — a direct line connects Mexican tomato cultivation to Neapolitan pizza.

## Flavor Profile & Pairings

A well-made Margherita is a study in balance: the bright acidity of the raw tomato sauce, the creamy richness of fresh mozzarella, the aromatic sweetness of fresh basil, and the chewy, slightly charred, yeasty crust. The olive oil adds fruitiness, and the flaky salt provides crystalline pops of seasoning.

Serve with a simple arugula salad dressed with lemon and olive oil — the peppery greens cut through the cheese's richness. A light Italian red like Barbera d'Asti or a crisp white like Vermentino are classic pairings. Cold Peroni or Moretti beer is equally appropriate.
