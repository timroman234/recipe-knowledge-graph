# Dan Dan Noodles (担担面)

**Cuisine:** Chinese | **Difficulty:** Medium | **Time:** 30 min | **Serves:** 4

Dan dan noodles are Sichuan's iconic street noodle — thin wheat noodles draped in a rich, creamy-spicy sauce of sesame paste, chili oil, and soy sauce, topped with crispy seasoned ground pork and crunchy preserved mustard greens. Every slurp delivers heat, nuttiness, and deep umami.

## Background & Origins

Dan dan mian (担担面) takes its name from the shoulder poles (dan dan) that street vendors once used to carry their portable noodle kitchens through the streets of Chengdu. One basket held the noodles and broth, the other held the toppings and sauces. The dish evolved from humble street food into one of the most celebrated noodle dishes in the world.

The structure of dan dan noodles — noodles + creamy/fatty sauce + seasoned ground meat — has a striking parallel in Italian carbonara: both combine a starch-based noodle with a rich, emulsified sauce and rendered cured/seasoned pork. The Italian version uses egg yolk and Pecorino for creaminess; the Chinese version uses sesame paste and chili oil. Both traditions independently discovered that noodles + creamy fat + savory pork is an irresistible combination.

The sesame paste sauce also connects to Mexican mole, which uses sesame seeds and ground peanuts for body and richness. The flavor profiles are entirely different, but the technique of using ground nuts and seeds to create a rich, clingy sauce is shared across cultures.

## Ingredients

### For the Sauce (per bowl)
- 1.5 tablespoons Chinese sesame paste (tahini is an acceptable substitute)
- 1 tablespoon chili oil with sediment
- 1 tablespoon soy sauce
- 2 teaspoons rice vinegar (or Chinkiang black vinegar)
- 1 teaspoon sugar
- 1/2 teaspoon Sichuan peppercorn powder
- 1 garlic clove, finely minced

### For the Pork Topping
- 8 oz ground pork
- 2 tablespoons vegetable oil
- 2 tablespoons Shaoxing wine
- 1 tablespoon soy sauce
- 2 tablespoons preserved mustard greens (ya cai or sui mi ya cai), rinsed and minced
- 1 teaspoon sugar

### For Serving
- 14 oz fresh Chinese wheat noodles (or dried thin wheat noodles)
- 3 scallions, thinly sliced
- 2 tablespoons roasted peanuts, crushed
- Extra chili oil to taste

## Instructions

1. **Cook the pork topping.** Heat vegetable oil in a wok or skillet over high heat. Add ground pork and cook, breaking into very small crumbles, for 4-5 minutes until browned and starting to crisp. Add Shaoxing wine, soy sauce, preserved mustard greens, and sugar. Stir-fry for 2 more minutes until fragrant and slightly caramelized. Set aside.

2. **Mix the sauce.** In each serving bowl, combine sesame paste, chili oil, soy sauce, rice vinegar, sugar, Sichuan peppercorn powder, and minced garlic. Stir to combine — it will look thick and uneven, which is fine.

3. **Cook the noodles.** Bring a large pot of water to a rolling boil. Cook noodles according to package directions until just al dente. Reserve 1/2 cup of the starchy noodle cooking water.

4. **Combine.** Add 2-3 tablespoons of hot noodle water to each sauce bowl and stir vigorously — the hot water melts the sesame paste and emulsifies the sauce into a creamy, clingy coating. Drain noodles and divide among the bowls. Toss vigorously to coat every strand in sauce.

5. **Top and serve.** Spoon the crispy pork topping over the noodles. Garnish with scallions and crushed peanuts. Drizzle with additional chili oil to taste. Serve immediately — toss again at the table before eating to redistribute the sauce.

## Chef's Notes

**Chinese sesame paste vs. tahini.** Chinese sesame paste is made from toasted sesame seeds and is darker, nuttier, and more intensely flavored than Middle Eastern tahini (made from raw sesame). If substituting tahini, add an extra teaspoon of sesame oil to compensate. Either way, the paste must be stirred well before measuring, as the oil separates.

**The noodle water trick.** Just like in Italian pasta (carbonara, cacio e pepe), starchy noodle cooking water is the secret emulsifier. It transforms the thick, clumpy sesame paste into a smooth, silky sauce that clings to each strand. Without it, the sauce sits at the bottom of the bowl.

**Preserved mustard greens (ya cai).** This fermented condiment adds a salty, slightly tangy crunch that is traditional and important for texture. If unavailable, Tianjin preserved vegetables or finely diced kimchi are reasonable substitutes.

**Building your own chili oil.** Good chili oil makes or breaks this dish. Homemade is best: heat vegetable oil to 275°F, pour over a mixture of Sichuan chili flakes, sesame seeds, and a pinch of five-spice powder. Let cool completely. The sediment (chili flakes at the bottom) is as important as the oil itself — always scoop both.

## Flavor Profile & Pairings

Dan dan noodles are rich, nutty, and complex. The sesame paste provides a creamy, nutty base that the chili oil cuts through with heat and smokiness. The soy sauce and preserved vegetables add layers of salt and umami. The Sichuan peppercorns contribute their characteristic numbing tingle. The crispy pork topping provides savory crunch, and the peanuts add another layer of nuttiness.

Pair with hot and sour soup for a classic Sichuan combination, or serve alongside steamed dumplings. A cold lager beer or chilled jasmine tea balances the heat. These noodles also work well as a late-night comfort food — they come together in 30 minutes and deliver maximum flavor.
