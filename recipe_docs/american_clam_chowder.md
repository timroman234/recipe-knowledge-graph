# New England Clam Chowder

**Cuisine:** American Comfort | **Difficulty:** Medium | **Time:** 55 min | **Serves:** 6

New England clam chowder is the crown jewel of northeastern American soups — a thick, creamy, hearty bowl loaded with tender clams, chunks of potato, and smoky salt pork in a rich milk-and-cream base. It should be warming, briny, and just thick enough to coat a spoon.

## Background & Origins

Clam chowder has been a staple of New England coastal communities since at least the early 1700s. The name likely derives from "chaudière," the French word for a large cooking pot — early settlers in Nova Scotia and Massachusetts made thick stews with whatever the sea provided. The New England cream-based version we know today solidified in the 1800s, battling with the tomato-based Manhattan variant for supremacy (a rivalry still ongoing).

The dish follows the universal pattern of great soups worldwide: a rich, slow-built base with starch and protein. Mexican pozole uses a chile-infused broth with hominy and pork. Chinese hot and sour soup uses a thickened broth with tofu and mushrooms. Italian minestrone builds a vegetable-rich broth with pasta or beans. Each tradition starts with aromatics, builds a flavorful liquid, and adds starch for body and protein for substance.

What distinguishes chowder is its use of dairy (cream and milk) as the base rather than clear broth — this creates a rich, velvety body that turns the soup into something almost stew-like.

## Ingredients

- 4 lbs littleneck or cherrystone clams (or 2 cans (6.5 oz each) chopped clams with juice)
- 4 oz salt pork or thick-cut bacon, diced into 1/4-inch pieces
- 2 tablespoons butter
- 1 large onion, diced
- 2 celery stalks, diced
- 3 garlic cloves, minced
- 3 tablespoons all-purpose flour
- 2 cups clam juice (bottled, or from steaming fresh clams)
- 1.5 lbs Yukon Gold potatoes, peeled and cut into 1/2-inch cubes
- 1.5 cups whole milk
- 1 cup heavy cream
- 2 bay leaves
- 3 sprigs fresh thyme (or 1/2 teaspoon dried)
- 1/4 teaspoon white pepper
- Kosher salt to taste
- Fresh chives or parsley for garnish
- Oyster crackers for serving

## Instructions

1. **Steam the clams (if using fresh).** Place scrubbed clams in a large pot with 1 cup of water. Cover and steam over high heat for 5-8 minutes, shaking the pot occasionally, until the shells open. Discard any that remain closed. Strain the cooking liquid through a fine-mesh sieve lined with cheesecloth (to catch sand) — this is your clam broth, and it's liquid gold. Remove clam meat from shells and chop roughly. Set aside. If using canned clams, drain and reserve the juice.

2. **Render the salt pork.** In a large, heavy-bottomed pot or Dutch oven, cook diced salt pork over medium heat for 6-8 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the fat has rendered and the pieces are golden and crispy. Remove the crispy bits with a slotted spoon and set aside for garnish. Leave the rendered fat in the pot.

3. **Build the base.** Add butter to the rendered fat. Add onion and celery, cooking for 5-6 minutes until softened but not browned. Add garlic and cook for 1 minute. Sprinkle flour over the vegetables and stir constantly for 2 minutes — this creates a roux that thickens the chowder. It works the same way as the roux in mac and cheese or a classic béchamel.

4. **Add liquids.** Gradually stir in clam juice, whisking to prevent lumps. Add potatoes, bay leaves, and thyme. Bring to a simmer and cook for 12-15 minutes until the potatoes are tender but not falling apart. The starch from the potatoes will also contribute to the chowder's body.

5. **Add dairy.** Reduce heat to medium-low. Pour in milk and cream. Stir gently to combine. Heat until the chowder is steaming but not boiling — boiling will cause the dairy to curdle. Add the chopped clams in the last 3-4 minutes of cooking (overcooking makes them rubbery). Season with white pepper and salt to taste.

6. **Rest.** Remove from heat, discard bay leaves and thyme sprigs. Let the chowder sit for 15-20 minutes before serving. Like many soups, chowder benefits from a brief rest — the flavors meld and the starch continues to thicken the base.

7. **Serve.** Ladle into deep bowls. Garnish with reserved crispy salt pork bits, fresh chives, and a crack of black pepper. Serve with oyster crackers or a hunk of crusty sourdough bread for dipping.

## Chef's Notes

**Don't boil the dairy.** Once you add the milk and cream, keep the heat gentle. Boiling causes cream to separate and can produce a grainy texture. A gentle simmer is all you need.

**Yukon Gold potatoes are the best choice.** They hold their shape during cooking (unlike Russets, which disintegrate into starch) while still contributing a creamy quality to the broth. Some recipes use Russets intentionally to thicken the chowder naturally — a valid approach, but you lose the satisfying potato chunks.

**Salt pork vs. bacon.** Salt pork is the traditional choice — it's unsmoked cured pork belly that renders clean, porky fat without adding smokiness. Bacon works as a substitute but adds a smoky note that changes the flavor profile. Both are excellent, just different. The use of cured pork fat as a flavor base connects to Italian carbonara (guanciale), Chinese dan dan noodles (rendered pork), and Mexican pozole (pork shoulder) — cured or slow-rendered pork is a universal flavor builder.

**Next-day chowder is better.** Like Mexican pozole and Italian osso buco, chowder improves overnight as flavors meld. The potatoes absorb more of the creamy broth, and the clam flavor permeates the entire dish. Reheat gently over low heat, adding a splash of milk if it's too thick.

## Flavor Profile & Pairings

New England clam chowder is briny, creamy, and deeply comforting. The clams provide a sweet, oceanic flavor, while the salt pork adds a smoky-savory depth. The potatoes give body and starchiness, and the cream creates a rich, velvety base that coats the mouth. The overall effect is like the sea wrapped in a warm blanket.

Serve with oyster crackers (their crunch and salt are the traditional accompaniment) or thick slices of sourdough bread. A crisp white wine like Muscadet or Sancerre pairs beautifully, as does a dry hard cider. Keep sides simple — a green salad with lemon vinaigrette is plenty.
