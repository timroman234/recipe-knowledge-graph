# Char Siu (Cantonese BBQ Pork)

**Cuisine:** Chinese | **Difficulty:** Easy | **Time:** 50 min (plus 4-hr marinade) | **Serves:** 6

Char siu is Cantonese barbecue pork at its most irresistible — strips of pork shoulder roasted until caramelized and lacquered with a sticky-sweet glaze of honey, hoisin, and five-spice. The edges char to a deep mahogany red while the interior stays juicy and tender. It's the glistening centerpiece of every Chinese BBQ shop window.

## Background & Origins

Char siu (叉燒) literally means "fork-roasted" — traditionally, long strips of marinated pork were skewered on forks and roasted over an open fire or in a cylindrical oven. The technique has been central to Cantonese cuisine for centuries, particularly in Guangdong province and Hong Kong, where specialized BBQ shops (siu laap) display rows of glistening roasted meats in their windows.

The combination of sweet glaze + slow-roasted pork connects char siu to several global BBQ traditions. Mexican carnitas achieve tenderness through fat-braising rather than dry-roasting, but share the goal of transforming pork shoulder into deeply flavored, caramelized meat. American smoked brisket and pulled pork use low-and-slow smoking to achieve similar results — different techniques, same principle of converting tough, collagen-rich cuts into tender, flavorful meat through patience and heat.

The distinctive red color of traditional char siu comes from red fermented bean curd or red food coloring — it's an aesthetic tradition, not a flavor component, and can be omitted for home cooking.

## Ingredients

### For the Marinade
- 2.5 lbs pork shoulder, cut into 2-inch thick strips (along the grain)
- 3 tablespoons hoisin sauce
- 3 tablespoons soy sauce
- 2 tablespoons honey (plus more for glazing)
- 2 tablespoons Shaoxing wine
- 1 tablespoon sesame oil
- 1 tablespoon oyster sauce
- 3 garlic cloves, minced
- 1 teaspoon five-spice powder
- 1/2 teaspoon white pepper
- Few drops red food coloring (optional, for traditional appearance)

### For the Glaze
- Reserved marinade (from the marinating bowl)
- 2 tablespoons honey
- 1 tablespoon soy sauce

## Instructions

1. **Marinate.** Combine all marinade ingredients in a large bowl or zip-top bag. Add pork strips and massage the marinade into the meat. Refrigerate for at least 4 hours, preferably overnight. Turn the meat once or twice during marinating.

2. **Prepare the glaze.** Remove pork from marinade, reserving the liquid. Pour reserved marinade into a small saucepan with additional honey and soy sauce. Simmer for 5 minutes until slightly thickened and syrupy.

3. **Roast.** Preheat oven to 425°F. Place a wire rack over a foil-lined sheet pan. Add 1 cup of water to the pan (this prevents the drippings from smoking). Arrange pork strips on the rack. Roast for 15 minutes.

4. **Glaze and flip.** Remove from oven, brush generously with glaze, and flip the strips. Brush the other side. Return to oven for 10 minutes.

5. **Final char.** Switch oven to broil. Brush with a final heavy coating of glaze. Broil for 3-5 minutes, watching carefully, until the edges caramelize and char slightly. The surface should be deeply lacquered and sticky. Let rest for 10 minutes.

6. **Slice and serve.** Slice char siu against the grain into 1/4-inch thick pieces. Drizzle any remaining glaze over the sliced meat. Serve with steamed rice, tucked into steamed buns, or sliced over egg noodle soup.

## Chef's Notes

**Pork shoulder is essential.** Lean cuts like tenderloin dry out during roasting. Pork shoulder (butt) has the perfect balance of fat and meat — the fat bastes the meat internally during cooking and the collagen keeps it moist. This is the same cut used in Mexican carnitas and American pulled pork, and for the same reason.

**Five-spice powder is the signature.** Chinese five-spice typically contains star anise, cloves, cinnamon, Sichuan peppercorn, and fennel seed. It provides the warm, aromatic complexity that distinguishes char siu from other BBQ traditions. The combination of sweet (honey, hoisin) and aromatic spice (five-spice) creates a flavor profile that bridges Asian and Western BBQ concepts.

**Don't skip the water in the pan.** The sugar in the marinade drips onto the foil and burns, creating acrid smoke. The water catches the drippings and prevents this. Replace the water if it evaporates during cooking.

**Versatility.** Char siu is one of the most versatile proteins in Chinese cooking. It's served over rice (char siu fan), in noodle soups, stuffed into steamed buns (char siu bao), added to fried rice, or used as a filling for dan dan noodles and wonton. Making a large batch and refrigerating it gives you a week's worth of instant meal additions.

## Flavor Profile & Pairings

Char siu is sweet, savory, and deeply caramelized. The hoisin and honey create a sticky sweetness, the soy sauce adds salt and umami, and the five-spice contributes warm, aromatic complexity. The charred edges provide a slight bitterness that prevents the sweetness from becoming cloying. The overall effect is rich and intensely flavored.

Steamed white rice is the classic accompaniment — its neutral canvas lets the char siu flavors shine. Stir-fried bok choy or Chinese broccoli provides a green, bitter counterpoint. For a complete Cantonese meal, serve alongside hot and sour soup or a simple egg drop soup.
